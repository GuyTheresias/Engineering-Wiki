History of Engineering

            Engineering is defined by ABET as: The profession in which a knowledge of the mathematical and natural sciences gained by study, experience, and practice is applied with judgment to develop ways to utilize economically, the materials and forces of nature for the benefit of mankind. Everything we see before us in the modern age has been to some degree engineered. Our homes, our cars, the roads and sidewalks, the water pipes, electric cables beneath our feet, and even radio signals shooting through the air. Such commodities a couple hundred years ago would seem like science fiction, and today they have become the norm. We owe this to the intellectual pursuits of engineers and the many fields of study they inhabit.

Primitive Engineering

            Before there were the modern commodities and the boon of the industrial ages, mankind was left bare against the elements. To protect the tribe and ensure their survival, innovation was crucial. Clothes were fashioned by animal hides and primitive housing was constructed for shelter against the elements. The men created weapons and tools so they could hunt and bring home more resources for their tribe to flourish, and crude wooden vessels allowed humanity to spread steadily across the width of the planet. While these developments happened before the term “engineering” was birthed, they were the humble beginnings of our tremendous ability to shape the world around us.

Early Cities and architectural innovations

            As humanity reached higher levels of knowledge in agriculture and animal husbandry, they began to settle down and establish themselves into cultural collectives. These we know today as the city. Jobs began to specialize, as it was no longer required for all the men to hunt for resources to sustain the tribe. More advanced tools were being made by the blacksmiths, forged from metal. Such as plows for farming, swords, axes, and many others. In Egypt, during around 2700 BC, the Egyptians set out to build the great pyramids, a logistical and engineering marvel for their time. With only rudimentary tools and pulleys, the Egyptians managed to build the pyramids over 400 feet tall made from massive stone blocks. Not only did they accomplish this task, but they still stand to this day.

            In Europe, the Greeks constructed another marvel, the Parthenon in 447 BC. It required extreme precision, as it was to honor their own gods with a temple of exquisite beauty. It was constructed of purely marble and was supported by finely partitioned marble columns that supported the weight of the building’s roof. This great structure like the pyramids, still stand to this day.

            As time ground on and cities grew larger and larger, so did their needs. The Romans, for example, needed methods to transport goods and resources across their vast empire. This need made the Romans great logisticians and transportation engineers. They constructed roads leading throughout their territories, so supplies and soldiers could move swiftly within Roman territories. Until 200 A.D when the Romans successfully connected every single one of their population centers with a road. The Romans also connected the cities to freshwater resources via aqueducts, which channeled water from a source directly into a Roman citizen’s home.

Industrial age

            When The Industrial Revolution began in the 1800’s, the world immediately changed. With machines being produced that greatly increased the pace in which resources could be gathered, refined, and reconstructed into new products. However, the industrial era created an entire new field of expertise that we know as industrial engineering. They optimize production, transportation, material usage, manpower, and much more. Essentially, it is their job to keep the complex systems of production running.

The Digital age

            The computer allowed unparalleled advancement in technology. The earliest computers were bulky and took up entire rooms. One man, Bernard M. Gordon saw the potential in computers and set to work innovating upon the design. Eventually he created the first commercial computer called UNIVAC and the first analog to digital converter. His innovations set forth the powerful devices we now see permeating our entire lives. With Money, entertainment, information, design, communication, and uncountable other concepts being digitalized.

            Today, the engineering field continues to grow, as people across the globe strive to innovate upon concepts and design new ones. From the early beginnings of civil and agricultural engineering to aerospace, the depth of knowledge in engineering will only grow larger.

Electrical Engineering Timeline

Click a button to scroll to the year

1745

Pieter van Musschenbroek and Ewald Georg von Kleist invented the Leyden jar. A device that allowed for the storage and continuous flow of electric charge. This allowed for experimentation with electricity.

1752

Benjamin Franklin discovered electrical charge with his experiment with his kite in a lightning storm.

1800

Alessandro Volta created the voltaic pile which preceded the modern type of battery we use today. It was another invention that allowed for a controlled flow of electricity that could be used for experiments that dealt with electric currents.

1820

Hans Christian Ørsted conducted many experiments and research that led to the invention of electric motors, inductors, and radio communication.

1821

Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction to generate current. The research and experimentation that Faraday conducted led to the production of energy through electromagnetic induction.

1831

Joseph Henry and Michael Faraday discovered electromagnetic induction and how it could be applied to create an electric motor.

1837

Thomas Davenport builds the first DC electric motor. This technology led to the development of other types of electric motors that evolved into what we have today.

1879

Thomas Edison and Joseph Swan invented the first incandescent light bulb. Possibly one of the most revolutionary inventions created, it allowed for the phasing out of kerosene lamps to electrical lighting around the world.

1888

Nikola Tesla invents alternating current (AC) as opposed to the widely used direct current (DC) which led to a whole different form of electrical technology. His inventions and experiments revolutionized the electrical engineering world.

1891

Nikola Tesla invents the induction motor, Tesla coil, AC power distribution, or wireless transmission of electricity. All of these were revolutionary for the age.

1896

Guglielmo Marconi demonstrates the first successful wireless telegraphy transmission over a distance of several miles leading to technological advancements in communication technology.

1904

John Ambrose Fleming invented the vacuum tube diode, this innovation led to modern electronics such as semiconductors and an introduction to more miniaturized technology.

1958

Jack Kilby invents the integrated circuit. This was another big step towards the miniaturization of modern technology that allowed for inventions such as smartphone technology.

1969

ARPANET is created. This was the precursor technology of the internet and was the first step towards a worldwide communications network.

1971

Intel was the first company to make microprocessors commercially available. This began the age of modern computing for the public that we know today.

1989

Tim Berners-Lee invents the World Wide Web, the most used technology to date. The internet on the world wide web led to the public ability to access information about nearly anything.

2007

Apple introduces the iPhone, putting smartphone technology on the map. This also revolutionized touchscreen technology and communication technology.
