Overview of Electrical Engineering

Introduction

            Electrical engineering was said to have emerged as a discipline in 1864, when James Clerk Maxwell, a Scottish physician summarized the basic laws of electricity in mathematical form and showed that radiation of electromagnetic energy travels through space at the speed of light. Maxwell then began to theorize ways to artificially recreate the discovered electromagnetic energies. Eventually a German physician named Heinrich Hertz in 1887 successfully created this phenomenon via radio waves.

            These scientific studies, combined with the industrial innovations of the telephone by Alexander Grahm Bell and Thomas Edison’s innovations such as the lightbulb and power plant combined to form the intellectual and practical groundwork for the electrical engineer. Knowledge on electrical theories and how to practically apply them for industrial use would become increasingly useful as nations built and expanded sophisticated power grids, and companies began to produce electronic appliances.

Related Fields

  • Test Engineer: responsible for conducting tests on electrical systems. When they identify problems during the tests, they consult their clients and offer guidance on implementing a solution. The average salary for a test engineer is $82,519 yearly.
  • Design Engineer: uses computer aided design to develop new systems and products. As a result, they work in close proximity to their clients to make sure their product meets the specifications required. The average salary for a design engineer is $86,519 yearly.
  • Communications Engineer: responsible for designing and developing communications systems such as satellites, radio towers, and fiber optic cables. They also assist with the installation of said communications equipment as well as assure to its quality. The average salary of a communications engineer is $99,134 yearly.
  • Aeronautical engineer: responsible for constructing systems for aircraft or satellites. They may design mechanical, communications, or electrical systems. It is their responsibility to perform rigorous tests to ensure that all the features of the craft are operating properly and in sync. On the design level their task is to evaluate ways to make improvements by observed aircraft performance. The average salary for an aeronautical Engineer is $102,155 yearly.
  • Electronics engineer: responsible for designing, developing, and implementing electronics equipment. This could vary from simple kitchen appliances to GPS systems or complex electric motors. They research designs, test components, and strive to meet the specifications required for their current projects. Electronics engineers also have to make sure that any components they are producing are compatible with other designs. The average salary of an Electronics engineer is $103,576 yearly.

Notable Electrical Engineers

Thomas Edison

            Edison grew up in the American Midwest, primarily Port Huron Michigan. Although he was not born there, the place of his birth was instead Milan Ohio in 1847. He was very interested in technology from a young age and as a young man he worked as a telegraph operator, familiarizing himself with the technology. Impoverished and living in a man’s basement in Elizibeth New jersey, Thomas Edison continued worked on the multiplex telegraphic system. This device would be his first financial success, allowing for two users to send messages simultaneously. He then produced the phonograph, an audio recording device that earned him widespread recognition for its novelty and ingenuity. What he is most known for today was the creation of the lightbulb. While he was not the first to ever attempt such a concept, his was by far the most effective, and so his breakthrough allowed electric light to be shined across the world.

William Shockley

            Born in London in 1910, William Shockley was raised by American parents in Palto Alto, California. He was homeschooled until eight years of age and learnt physics from his next-door neighbor who was a Stanford physics professor. He achieved his Bachelor of Science degree from Caltech in 1932 and a PhD from MIT in 1936. Shortly after, he became the director of research at Bell Labs later in the same year. He worked to find alternatives to vacuum tubes when his work was interrupted by the Second World War, and he was enlisted to help the US government on a various series of wartime projects. These included, anti-submarine warfare research, radar bomb sights for bomber aircraft, and casualty estimates for an invasion of the Japanese mainland.

            After the war Bell Labs formed a solid states physics group led by Shockley and chemist Stanley Morgan to finally put an end to the reign of feeble vacuum tube amplifiers. In the winter of 1946, after much trial and error, the transistor had been developed. The device being capable of regulating and amplifying electrical flow in a much more efficient and compact manner than the vacuum tubes could ever achieve.

Robert Noyce

            Born on December 12, 1927, in Burlington, Iowa, Noyce had a spark of curiosity from a young age. He and his brother made a glider in the summer of 1940 which they used to fly from the top of the Grinnell College stables. On more ridiculous happenstance was when he mechanized his sled by welding a propeller and an old washing machine motor to it. While quite dangerous, I can see such a mechanized monstrosity being very entertaining and incredibly fun. Noyce would attend Grinnell College in 1945 and in his junior year he almost got imprisoned for stealing the pig of the mayor and roasting it at the school’s Luau. He instead was suspended for just one semester and sentenced to compensate the mayor. The only reason he was let off so lightly was due to his great academic abilities, as the college felt it would be a tragedy to lose a bright mind to such shenanigans. He would go on top get a doctorate in physics from MIT in 1953.

            He went on to be recruited by William Shockley in 1956, but within a year he and many others became very jaded with Shockley’s authoritarian management styles. Eventually banding with a squad of traitors known fittingly as “The Traitorous Eight”. These renegades left Shockley Semiconductor to its fate and started the Fairchild Semiconductor corporation. Within this corporation Jack Kilby invented the first hybrid integrated circuit. Noyce would then go on to perfect the concept with the invention of his Monolithic Integrated Circuit, which could be massed produced unlike Kilby’s design due to the components being contained within the circuit.

Electrical Engineering Professional Societies

The IEEE

            The IEEE stands for: Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers. They are a very large organization consisting of over 460,000 members in more than 190 countries. These members are broken into many local chapters of university students, professionals, and affinity groups. The IEEE itself has 39 technical societies and eight technical councils that represent a range of IEEE interests. It is one of the world’s largest technical professional organizations. The IEEE has a vast documental archive that can be accessed via members and is involved in over 1000 current projects. The IEEE also sponsors many conferences and events in multiple countries, the papers produced from these conferences can also be found in the digital archives. When you sign up with a paid membership you get access to your local chapter and the available data archives of the IEEE. The IEEE also creates technological standards to ensure that the many electronic devices we use can communicate with each other. Overall, the IEEE’s mission is to advance technological standards for the betterment of humanity, as they say themselves.
